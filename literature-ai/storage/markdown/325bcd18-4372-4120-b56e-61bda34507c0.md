ABSTRACT: High-entropy alloy (HEA) nanoparticles offer unique catalytic properties due to their complex surface coordination and widely tunable electronic structures. Conventional synthesis methods typically involve extreme thermal shock ( ∼ 1700 ° C) to achieve metal coreduction and mixing. While wetchemical approaches hold potential for controlling nanoparticle properties, they are hindered by disparities in metal reduction kinetics and a diminished influence of configurational entropy on metal mixing at low temperatures, leading to phase segregation and limited compositional tunability. In this work, we introduce a novel wet-chemical hydrothermal method that enables the synthesis of HEA nanoparticles with enhanced compositional homogeneity and precise property control at low temperatures ( ∼ 170 ° C). ## ■ INTRODUCTION

High-entropy alloy (HEA) nanoparticles represent a novel class of materials with distinctive catalytic properties. 1 -4 Comprising at least five principal elements, HEA nanoparticles offer exceptional versatility in tuning electronic structures, surpassing traditional monometallic and few-element alloy nanoparticles. 3 The complex coordination environments on the HEA surfaces allow the binding of the initial and transition states of reaction intermediates to different metal atoms, potentially disrupting universal scaling relationships and accelerating reaction kinetics. 5 -7 Additionally, the highentropy composition may retard atomic diffusion within the nanoparticles, enhancing resistance to metal leaching and therefore improving catalytic stability under harsh conditions. 8,9 These unique characteristics position HEA nanoparticle as a promising platform for the development of advanced catalysts across a wide range of reactions.

To date, the most successful synthesis of HEA nanoparticles has been achieved through a thermal shock approach pioneered by Hu and colleagues (Figure 1, top panel). 10 -16 This method involves flash electric Joule heating of mixed metal precursors impregnated on a carbon support to extremely high temperatures ( ∼ 1700 ° C), creating intrinsically off-equilibrium conditions that promote the coreduction of the metal precursors and subsequent nucleation and growth of HEA nanoparticles. 2,16 Central to this process is an entropydriven alloying mechanism. Elevated temperatures ( T ) increase the contribution of configurational entropy ( Δ S mix &gt; 0) to the overall mixing free energy of metals ( Δ G mix = Δ H mix -T Δ S mix ). This ensures that Δ G mix becomes significantly negative, making the formation of the HEA thermodynamically favorable. As a result, this approach, along with analogous methods such as thermal and microwave heating, 17 -23 enables the creation of a diverse library of HEA nanoparticles with remarkable control over nanoparticle sizes and compositions.

thermodynamic equilibrium, offering high control over size, shape, and scalability for industrial production (Figure 1, bottom panel). 24 -28 This method is particularly effective for synthesizing monometallic metal nanoparticles. However, when targeting alloy nanoparticles, wet-chemical methods face significant challenges. Kinetically, the differing reduction potentials ( E 0 ) of metal precursors lead to nonsynchronized reduction, resulting in alloy nanoparticles with nonuniform elemental distributions and narrow compositional windows. 29 -34 This issue extends to HEA nanoparticles. 33 -46 Besides, the wet-chemical synthesis of HEA nanoparticles is also constrained by thermodynamic factors. The low synthesis temperatures ( T ) reduce the contribution of configurational entropy ( Δ S mix) to the overall mixing free energy of metals ( Δ G mix ), often resulting in a positive Δ G mix , which further promotes phase segregation.

Here, we present a novel wet-chemical hydrothermal method for synthesizing HEA nanoparticles by creating a localized off-equilibrium environment within a low-temperature, near-equilibrium wet-chemical system (Figure 1, bottom-right panel). This approach enables precise compositional control and enhanced alloy homogeneity of HEA nanoparticles. The synthesis utilizes active hydrogen (H · , i.e., hydrogen atoms or radicals), generated in situ through a metalcatalyzed organic dehydrogenation reaction confined to nuclei/seed surfaces, as a reducing agent. The significantly lower standard reduction potential of H · ( E 0 = -2.31 V) than that of many noble and non-noble metals ( E 0 usually &gt; -1 V) establishes an off-equilibrium environment that enables the synchronized reduction of various metal precursors as they diffuse to the nuclei/seed surface, irrespective of their intrinsic reduction potentials. 47,48 This serves as a kinetic driving force for homogeneous metal mixing in HEA nanoparticles, on the condition that no significant dynamic phase segregation occurs during the nanoparticle growth. The localization of this offequilibrium environment at the metal/solution interface further preserves the wet-chemical characteristics of the synthesis system, allowing for precise control over nanoparticle properties. Atomic-resolution elemental analysis reveals uniform distributions of the alloy components within individual nanoparticles, although exceptions exist that exhibit degrees of atomic-scale heterogeneity. This validates the efficacy of our wet-chemical approach, even at a low temperature of 170 ° C, in enhancing the mixing of metals within the nanoparticles. Each metal composition can be independently and precisely tuned over a broad range by adjusting the feed precursors. Based on this precise synthesis, we demonstrate the excellent catalytic performance of PtCuNiCoFe HEA nanoparticles in the electrocatalytic methanol oxidation reaction (MOR), which exceeds that of their monometallic and few-element alloy counterparts. The enhanced MOR performance is believed to be attributed to the diverse surface coordination environments and broad tunability of the electronic structures. This work introduces a new route to HEA nanoparticles that show precise nanoparticle properties including size and composition, thus opening new avenues for developing advanced catalysts for various important processes.

## ■ RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

Design of the Synthesis. We introduce H · confined on nuclei/seed surfaces to create a localized nonequilibrium environment within a near-equilibrium wet-chemical system. This strategy mitigates the influence of differing reduction potentials among metal precursors, enabling their synchronized reduction to form HEA nanoparticles with enhanced compositional homogeneity. Traditional methods for producing H · in wet-chemical systems have been limited. While we previously identified nitrous acid (HNO2) as a source of H · on metal surfaces, 47 its strong oxidizing nature may cause undesired side reactions, such as oxidation -reduction cycles of non-noble metals, especially in non-noble-metal-rich HEA nanoparticles, leading to phase segregation. 48 To avoid involving oxidizing species, we propose harvesting H · from organic dehydrogenation reactions  a widely used organic conversion process that releases hydrogen gas (H 2 ). 49 We hypothesize that H · exists as a key intermediate in the formation of H 2 and, if it persists long enough, can be captured by metal precursors for their coreduction. Among various organic dehydrogenation reactions, we specifically select those catalyzed by metals, ensuring that H · forms exclusively on metal surfaces. By coupling metalcatalyzed organic dehydrogenation with metal reduction, we establish a localized nonequilibrium environment, facilitating the synthesis of HEA nanoparticles via a wet-chemical approach.

Specifically, we chose the dehydrogenation of N, N -dimethylformamide (DMF) to generate interfacial H · . DMF has been reported to react with H 2 O, typically on a Cu catalyst surface, to produce H2: (CH3)2NCHO + H2O → (CH3)2NCOOH + H2 ↑ . 50 DMF and its products are mild toward non-noble metals, making it an ideal H · precursor for the controlled synthesis of HEA nanoparticles. In a typical synthesis, we mix DMF, H2O, metal precursors, a coordinating ligand (SCN -), a pH modulator (benzoic acid), and Vulcan XC-72 carbon black in an autoclave, heating the mixture at 170 ° C for 4 h. SCN -coordinates to the metal salts, preventing their direct reduction by DMF, a weak reducing agent, in the solution phase (Figure S1). Benzoic acid, a high-boiling-point organic acid, maintains an acidic environment at 170 ° C, inhibiting metal salt hydrolysis (Figure S2). At the initial stage, sparse metal nuclei may form on the carbon surface due to the reduction of noble metal precursors by DMF. This leads to an accelerated generation of H · through an autocatalytic process. The increased H · concentration promotes the formation of new nuclei on the carbon surface and initiates the H · -mediated interfacial reduction mechanism. This process ultimately results in the formation of supported HEA nanoparticles as heterogeneous catalysts.

Characterizations of HEA Nanoparticles. We demonstrate the effectiveness of our strategy by synthesizing carbonsupported senary Pt13 Ir10Cu23In14Ga26Pb14 HEA nanoparticles (Figures 2, S3, and S4; E 0 for PtCl6 2 -, Ir 3+ , Cu 2+ , In 3+ , Ga 3+ , and Pb 2+ , + 0.68, + 1.156, + 0.342, -0.338, -0.549, and -0.126 eV, respectively). High-angle annular dark-field scanning transmission electron microscopy (HAADF-STEM) revealed that a high density of HEA nanoparticles formed on the carbon black, with no free colloidal nanoparticles observed (Figure 2a). This result suggests that the proposed synthesis mechanism can occur on the carbon surface through heterogeneous nucleation, enabling the direct synthesis of supported HEA nanoparticles without the need for organic surfactants. SCN -, while used as a ligand during the synthesis process, can be easily desorbed from noble metal sites by electrochemical treatments (Figure S5). This ensures that the synthesized HEA nanoparticles have highly accessible surfaces. The abundant functional groups on carbon, such as -OH, may contribute to the metal/carbon interaction for heterogeneous nucleation and formation of well-dispersed HEA nanoparticles. The average size of the HEA nanoparticles is 1.9 nm (Figure 2a, inset) and can be tuned between 1 -4 nm by controlling the metal precursor/carbon ratio. The ultrasmall size is also reflected in the X-ray diffraction (XRD) pattern, which shows a broad peak indicating short atomic periodicity (Figure 2b). The ultrasmall size facilitates not only the exposure of metal atoms but also the optimization of catalytic performance through the size effect, as nanoparticles within this size range (typically 1 -4 nm) often exhibit distinctive catalytic properties. 51 -53 It is worth noting that the synthesis of HEA nanoparticles does not rely on carbon black. As a demonstration, these HEA nanoparticles can successfully grow on alternative substrates such as TiO2 nanoparticles (Figure S6). Notably, when synthesized without any support, the process yields ultrathin HEA nanowires as a colloid (Figure S7).

The structural features of the senary PtIrCuInGaPb HEA nanoparticles are revealed at the atomic scale via spherical aberration ( Cs )-corrected HAADF-STEM (Figure 2c; inset: Fourier diffractogram). The crystal structure can be determined to be face-centered cubic. Instead of a highly ordered crystal structure, the HEA nanoparticles exhibit clear deviations of metal atoms from their equilibrium positions, resulting in substantial lattice distortion. This lattice distortion is evidenced by the cyan line in the image, which shows a zigzag arrangement of metal atoms instead of a straight line in a perfect lattice. The atomic spacing also varies significantly. Along the yellow line in the image, the spacing between adjacent metal atoms ranges from 1.95 to 2.77 Å, showing a deviation of approximately ± 16% from the average spacing of 2.40 Å. The lattice distortion can be attributed to the large differences in the sizes of the metal atoms in the HEA nanoparticles. 4,36 This distortion may hinder the diffusion of metal atoms throughout the nanoparticle, thus improving chemical stability under harsh conditions.

This synthesis can incorporate multiple metals, both noble and non-noble, into ultrasmall nanoparticles, resulting in broad compositional windows despite distinct elemental characteristics. The elemental fractions of Pt, Ir, Cu, In, Ga, and Pb in the senary HEA nanoparticles were determined to be 13%, 10%, 23%, 14%, 26%, and 14%, respectively, via energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) (Figure 2d). These values closely match those measured by inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) (Table S1). These results suggest successful alignment of the reduction rates of nonnoble metals with those of noble metals, leading to effective alloying in these ultrasmall nanoparticles. The fraction of nonnoble metals can be further tuned to &gt;80% (discussed later in compositional control), highlighting the broad compositional windows of the HEA nanoparticles achieved by this strategy, which significantly surpasses the capabilities of conventional wet-chemical approaches. This broad compositional window enhances the regulation of the electronic structure of the HEA nanoparticles, offering opportunities for improved catalytic properties.

The HEA nanoparticles exhibit a high degree of elemental distribution homogeneity. Figure 2e presents atomic-scale elemental mappings of a representative PtIrCuInGaPb HEA nanoparticle acquired by electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS) on a Cs -corrected transmission electron microscope. All metallic components demonstrate a uniform distribution across the entire nanoparticle, as evidenced by the welloverlapping spectrum images. Although some noise is apparent in the Ga signal, the overall trend indicates excellent elemental mixing. We also observed slight segregation of metal components in certain nanoparticles, which aligns with previous findings by Skrabalak et al. regarding potential intraparticle heterogeneity. 54 This phenomenon may have been underestimated in many prior STEM-EDS studies due to the technique's limited spatial resolution and challenges in determining 3D elemental distributions from 2D projections. However, the enhanced intraparticle homogeneity observed in this work using C s -corrected atomic-resolution STEM-EELS validates the efficacy of our H · -mediated interfacial reduction mechanism in promoting metal mixing. The PtCuNiCoFe HEA was also obtained as an ultrathin epitaxial coating on preexisting metal nanocrystals (Figure S12). While synchronized metal reduction is generally effective in promoting metal mixing within HEA nanoparticles, there may be limitations when the mixing free energy ( Δ G mix) is significantly positive at the low synthesis temperature, depending on the miscibility of metal components. 55 Therefore, atomic migration may occur across the crystal growth front during metal deposition, leading to local compositional heterogeneity within the nanoparticles. Nevertheless, our synthesis method demonstrates substantial potential for producing homogeneous HEA nanoparticles through wetchemical systems at significantly reduced temperatures compared to conventional methods, 56,57 thereby offering enhanced control over nanoparticle properties. Besides, this wet-chemical approach is scalable, presenting the potential for mass-production of HEA nanoparticles in practical applications (Figure S13).

Precise Compositional Control. This approach leverages the strengths of both nonequilibrium and wet-chemical synthesis techniques. The wet-chemical system ensures consistent metal precursor concentrations surrounding all the nanoparticles, promoting uniform growth conditions. Concurrently, the localized nonequilibrium environment generated by the H · -mediated reduction process effectively overcomes the disparity in reduction potentials among metal precursors, facilitating their synchronized reduction. Consequently, this synthesis enables precise control over the composition of the HEA nanoparticles.

To demonstrate its compositional control capability, we synthesized senary PtIrCuInGaPb HEA nanoparticles with varying compositions by systematically adjusting the concentration of a single metal precursor while keeping the other concentrations constant (Figure 3). The resulting HEA nanoparticle compositions were analyzed through EDS, with each metal content normalized to a specific noble metal component (Pt or Ir) for comparison (Figures S14, S15). When the Pt/Ir ratio in the precursor was varied from 0 to 1.2, the Pt/Ir ratio in the HEA nanoparticles shifted from 0 to 1.4, showing a clear linear relationship (correlation constant, 0.995) (Figure 3a, bottom panel). This linearity demonstrates the precise control over the Pt content in the HEA nanoparticles achieved by adjusting its precursor concentration. Notably, changes in the Pt precursor concentration had a minimal effect on the contents of other metals (Cu, Pb, In, Ga, and Ir) in the HEA nanoparticles, suggesting that the metal reduction processes do not interfere with one another, allowing for independent control of the metal contents in the HEA nanoparticles (Figure 3a, top panel). Similar control was observed for the other five components in the HEA nanoparticles, confirming the versatility of our synthesis strategy in fully manipulating the composition of HEA nanoparticles (Figure 3b -f). Furthermore, the compositional control capability of our approach was validated in a quinary PtCuNiCoFe HEA system (Figures S16 and S17), demonstrating precise and independent control over the compositions of these quinary HEA nanoparticles.

Such precise and independent compositional control of metal nanoparticles, including but not limited to HEAs, is uncommon in conventional wet-chemical synthesis, especially through one-pot protocols. 58,59 Typically, compositional control is hindered by significant differences in the reduction potentials of metal precursors. 46 The reduction of one metal can interfere with the reduction of another, making it difficult to adjust the content of one metal without affecting the others. 60,61 Our strategy overcomes these limitations, offering a valuable tool for the tailored synthesis of HEA nanoparticles for catalyst screening in diverse applications.

Synthesis Mechanism Study. We propose harvesting H · from metal-catalyzed organic dehydrogenation reactions for the controlled synthesis of HEA nanoparticles (proposed mechanism, Figure 4a). To confirm the presence of H · , we introduced 5,5-dimethyl-1-pyrroline N -oxide (DMPO), a radical trapping agent, into a model synthesis system comprising DMF+H2O+H2PtCl6. The solution was subjected to hydrothermal treatment at 170 ° C and consequently analyzed via electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) (Figure 4b). Intense signals corresponding to spin-trapped radicals (DMPO-H · ) were detected, with splitting constants of α N = 15.7 G and α H = 21.3 G. These results unequivocally confirm the intermediacy of H · in the DMF dehydrogenation reaction during the hydrothermal synthesis process. To ascertain whether H · forms on a catalytic metal surface, we conducted comparative control experiments with and without catalytic metals (Figure 4c). Following hydrothermal treatments of both the DMF+H2O+Pt/C and the DMF+H2O+H2PtCl6 systems (where H2PtCl6 is reduced to metallic Pt), substantial quantities of H2 were detected by mass spectrometry (MS) at m / z = 2 within the reaction chamber. This H 2 formation is attributed to the combination of H · on the Pt surfaces.

We further investigated the origin of H · in this synthesis system. To determine whether H2O contributes to H · formation, we introduced isotope-labeled D2O into a model reaction system (DMF+D2O+Pt/C) and analyzed the gaseous product via MS (Figure 4d). A significant amount of HD was detected as the main gaseous product at m / z = 3, suggesting that H2 forms by combining one H atom from DMF and one from H2O. In essence, DMF and H2O each release one H ·

during the dehydrogenation reaction, and these H · combine to form a H2 molecule. Two minor MS peaks were detected at m / z = 2 and 4, corresponding to H2 and D2, respectively. These byproducts can be attributed to the combination of two H · or two D · from separate dehydrogenation reactions. The formation of HD is more probable than H2 or D2, explaining the prominence of the HD peak in the MS data. Based on the above results, the mechanism for H · formation can be proposed as follows (Figure 4a): DMF adsorbs onto a metal surface ( * ), activating its C -H bond in the aldehyde group to form * (CH3)2NCO and * H (Reaction 1). Meanwhile, H2O adsorbs at adjacent metal sites, forming * OH and * H (Reaction 2). The * OH and * (CH3)2NCO in proximity then react to form (CH3)2NCOOH. The * H, i.e., H · adsorbed on the metal surface, can either participate in further metal reduction reactions or combine to form H2 when present in excess (Reaction 3).

The critical role of interfacial H · in achieving synchronized metal reduction was further corroborated (Figure 4g, h). First, we confirmed that the aldehyde group in DMF, which possesses weak reducing capacity, is insufficient for synchronized metal reduction. To demonstrate this, we synthesized senary PtIrCuInGaPb HEA nanoparticles using aldehyde analogs of DMF, such as formaldehyde (HCHO) and acetaldehyde (CH3CHO), which do not undergo dehydrogenation under experimental conditions, as substitutes. Among the six elements, only Pt, Ga, and Cu were detected in the nanoparticles, indicating sequential metal reduction (Figure 4g). This result starkly contrasts with the synthesis using DMF, highlighting the pivotal role of interfacial H · , formed by DMF dehydrogenation, in achieving synchronized metal reduction during the wet-chemical synthesis. Second, the influence of DMF concentration on HEA nanoparticle formation was investigated. In a typical synthesis of PtIrCuInGaPb HEA nanoparticles, effective metal coreduction was only achieved when the DMF volume exceeded 30 μ L. At lower volumes, the nanoparticle yield was significantly reduced, and the products contained only 2 to 3 types of metals (Figure S18). These results confirm that DMF plays a critical role in enabling effective metal coreduction for controlled HEA nanoparticle synthesis, supporting our H · -mediated interfacial reduction mechanism. It is worth noting that DMF can decompose to release CO at high temperatures (experimental evidence, Figure S19). 62,63 The continuous generation of CO from DMF decomposition may have contributed to the metal reduction reactions, potentially aiding the H · -mediated interfacial reduction mechanism. Finally, we analyzed the rates of metal reduction during H · -mediated nonequilibrium reduction synthesis by examining the compositions of the HEA nanoparticles at various reaction times (Figure 4h). By 0.5 h, the fractions of all the metal components exceeded 5% and remained virtually stable from 1 to 4 h. The observed changes in Cu and Ga fractions between 0.5 and 1 h are likely due to experimental deviations caused by the small amounts of metals being reduced in the early stages of the synthesis. These results suggest that effective metal coreduction occurred throughout the synthesis process, ensuring compositional homogeneity within individual HEA nanoparticles.

Electrochemical MOR on HEA Surfaces. Our synthesis enables the precise fabrication of HEA nanoparticles with highly complex surface coordination environments, which allows the disruption of the scaling relationship between the adsorption energies of intermediates involved in multistep reactions, leading to enhanced overall kinetics. 3,4,64 To demonstrate this effect, we synthesized PtNiCoCuFe HEA nanoparticles (Figure S20) and evaluated their activities in the electrocatalytic methanol oxidation reaction (MOR), a typical multistep process (Figure 5). The activities of commercial Pt/ C and few-element alloy nanoparticles including PtNi, PtNiCo, and PtNiCoCu (Figure S21; synthesis conditions, Table S2), were also investigated for comparison. As it is impractical to investigate all possible compositions, we selected representative compositions, ensuring nearly equal metal contents in these catalysts and maintaining the noble/non-noble metal ratios close to 1. Before the electrochemical tests, the nanoparticles were activated by cyclic voltammetry (CV) scanning in the potential range of 0.05 -1.1 V vs the reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE) for 300 cycles in N2-saturated 0.5 M H2SO4, effectively removing the SCN -ligand from the Pt active sites without significantly altering the nanoparticle composition (Figure S22). Figure 5a shows the MOR performance of these nanoparticles in N2-saturated 0.5 M H2SO4 + 1 M CH3OH. The specific MOR activities, defined as the peak current densities normalized to the respective electrocatalytically active surface areas (ECSAs) (Figure S23, Table S3), increase in the order of Pt/C (0.7 mA cm -2 ) &lt; Pt55Ni45 (2.7 mA cm -2 ) &lt; Pt54Ni27Co19 (3.7 mA cm -2 ) &lt; Pt38Ni21Co12Cu29 (5.6 mA cm -2 ) &lt; Pt42Ni15Co8Cu28Fe7 (8.0 mA cm -2 ). The PtNiCoCuFe HEA nanoparticles exhibited the highest specific activity, significantly surpassing that of monometallic Pt and few-element alloy nanoparticles, placing them among the most active catalysts developed to date (comparison with reported catalysts, Table S4). 65 -69 Moreover, the PtNiCoCuFe HEA nanoparticles also demonstrated the highest mass activity (1.96 A mg Pt -1 ), i.e., the peak current density normalized to the Pt mass, among all the catalysts investigated, suggesting their potential for practical applications (Figure S23). These results highlight the effectiveness of HEA surfaces in enhancing multistep catalytic reactions.

The PtNiCoCuFe HEA nanoparticles exhibited enhanced catalytic stability in the MOR. During an accelerated deterioration test (ADT), the Pt42Ni15Co8Cu28Fe7 HEA nanoparticles retained 84% of their initial current density after 1,000 cycles of potential sweep (0.05 -1.2 V at 100 mV

s -1 ). This performance surpassed that of monometallic Pt/C and few-element Pt55Ni45 and Pt38Ni21Co12Cu29 alloy nanoparticles, which retained 54%, 56%, and 72% of their initial current densities, respectively (Figure 5b; additional results, Figures S23, S24). Two key factors contribute to the high catalytic stability of these HEA nanoparticles. First, the high configurational entropy enhances the compositional stability of the HEA nanoparticles during catalysis (Figure 5c). After 1,000 ADT cycles, the Pt42Ni15Co8Cu28Fe7 HEA nanoparticles experienced a non-noble metal loss of only ∼ 34.5%. In contrast, the non-noble metal loss in Pt55Ni45 nanoparticles reached 78% under the same conditions. EELS mapping revealed that the postcatalytic HEA nanoparticles retained a high degree of metal mixing, although a partial transformation of the random alloy phase into an intermetallic structure was observed (Figure S25). These results suggest that the enhanced chemical stability is closely related to the increasing configurational entropy of the alloy nanoparticles, likely due to improved compositional homogeneity and significant lattice distortion. Second, the binding of SCN -at non-noble metal sites suppressed oxidative etching of the HEA nanoparticles. SCN -binds weakly at Pt sites, allowing easy removal to expose active sites, but binds strongly to non-noble metal sites, leading to passivation of vulnerable sites (Figures S5 and S22). For comparison, Pt44Ni29Co12Cu7Fe8 HEA nanoparticles with clean surfaces (Figure S26) retained only 33% of their original MOR activity (Figure S27) and experienced a significant nonnoble metal loss (final composition: Pt79Ni13Co2Cu4Fe2; Figure S28) during 700 ADT cycles. This contrast highlights the critical role of SCN -passivation in improving the compositional stability of the HEA nanoparticles.

Theoretical Validations. Density functional theory (DFT) calculations were conducted to explore the relationship between the enhanced catalytic performance in the MOR and the high-entropy atomic arrangements on nanoparticle surfaces (Figure 5d -h). A model was constructed to simulate the Sdecorated Pt42Ni15Co8Cu28Fe7 HEA nanoparticles based on the minimal surface energy configuration (Figure S29). A monometallic Pt model was also constructed for comparison. The binding energies of * OH and * CO, two key descriptors for MOR, 70,71 on both surfaces were investigated (Figure 5d -g). An efficient MOR requires strong * OH binding but weak * CO binding, which is difficult to achieve with single metals such as Pt due to the scaling relationship, when both bindings tend to or decrease simultaneously increase (Figure 5e,g). HEAs provide a variety of adsorption sites due to their complex atomic arrangements, leading to significant variations in local adsorption potential energy across different sites. Indeed, on the HEA surface, * OH and * CO exhibited strong binding at the Fe, Co, and Ni sites, while showing weaker binding at the Pt and Cu sites (Figure 5d,5f). Figure 5i presents the average adsorption energies ( G ads ) of * OH and * CO on Pt42Ni15Co8Cu28Fe7 HEA and monometallic Pt surfaces, derived from the respective adsorption potential energy mappings (Figure 5d -g). On average, compared with the monometallic Pt surface, the HEA surface had a significantly greater * OH binding energy and lower * CO binding energy, which jointly enhanced the overall MOR kinetics. Based on both theory and experiment, an intermediate migration mechanism is proposed (Figure 5h). On the HEA surface, CH3OH undergoes a series of dehydrogenation reactions to form * CO preferentially at Fe/Co/Ni sites with high * OH binding energies. * CO then migrates to Pt/Cu sites with low * CO binding energies, where it is oxidized by * OHat adjacent Fe/Co/Ni sites, facilitated by H2O dissociation at these sites. Such a mechanism, facilitated by locally varying adsorption sites of HEAs, can well explain the significantly improved MOR efficiency as observed experimentally (Figure 5a).

The diverse catalytic sites on the HEA surface are formed due to the effective tuning of local electronic structures through multimetallic interactions. The projected partial density of states (PDOS) reveals evident overlaps among the d orbitals of the metals, indicating strong bonding between these elements (Figure S30). Bader analysis reveals that the excess electron density of the outmost surface atoms increases in the order of Fe &lt; Co, Ni, Cu &lt; Pt, suggesting a sequential electron transfer in this order (Figure 5j). Thus, the unique electronic structure of the HEA nanoparticles can be attributed to electron transfer among the multimetallic components. These electronic interactions align with experimental observations. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) confirmed electron transfer from non-noble metals to Pt in the HEA nanoparticles (Figure S31, S32). Electrochemical CO stripping shows that with an increasing number of non-noble metal components in the HEA nanoparticles, the CO oxidation peak shifts continuously to lower potentials, indicating increasing electron density at the Pt sites and thereby weakening CO adsorption energy (Figure S33). Therefore, efficient electron transfer among the multimetallic components significantly impacts the electronic structure of the HEA nanoparticles, contributing to the enhanced MOR activity.

## ■ CONCLUSIONS

In summary, we have developed a novel strategy for synthesizing HEA nanoparticles with precisely customizable compositions and enhanced compositional homogeneity in a wet-chemical system, which is a substantial improvement over conventional methods. To achieve this, we harnessed H · , a highly reductive species generated from the DMF dehydrogenation reaction on nuclei/seed surfaces, to establish a localized off-equilibrium reduction environment within the nearequilibrium system. On the one hand, the localized offequilibrium environment ensures synchronized reduction of different types of metal precursors in these specific zones, leading to enhanced metal mixing in HEA nanoparticles, overcoming the thermodynamic tendency toward phase segregation and kinetic gaps in metal reduction governed by their intrinsic reduction potentials, surpassing conventional wet-chemical synthesis. Notably, while some nanoparticles exhibited compositional heterogeneity, we observed homogeneous HEA nanoparticles with full metal mixing at 170 ° C, a temperature significantly lower than that required for thermal shock synthesis. On the other hand, the wet-chemical nature of our method allowed for precise manipulation of nanoparticle properties, such as size and composition. We demonstrated independent control over compositions, including noble and non-noble metals, in HEA nanoparticles with unparalleled precision, surpassing conventional thermal shock synthesis. Owing to this precision synthesis, we further demonstrated the effectiveness of the controlled surface local coordination environment and electronic structure of PtCuNiCoFe HEA nanoparticles in enhancing the electrocatalytic activity in the MOR. This work offers a new avenue for the synthesis of HEA nanoparticles with improved elemental homogeneity and controlled nanoparticle properties, potentially applicable to the synthesis of HEA derivatives, such as oxides, chalcogenides, and phosphides with tailored properties, thus opening new opportunities for the development of advanced catalysts in a broad range of catalytic applications.

## ■ EXPERIMENTAL SECTION

Synthesis of Carbon-Supported HEA and Few-Element Alloy Nanoparticles. Carbon black was pretreated to graft hydroxyl groups for enhanced interactions with metal nanoparticles. 72 In brief, 5 g of Vulcan XC-72 carbon black was dispersed in a mixed acid including 75 mL of H2SO4 and 25 mL of HNO3, and stirred for 12 h. The resulting hydroxyl-functionalized carbon black (OH-XC-72) was recovered by centrifugation, washed with H 2 O until pH neutral, dried at 60 ° C, and ground into fine powders using an agate mortar. In a typical synthesis of Pt13 Ir10Cu23In14Ga26Pb14 HEA nanoparticles, metal salts [6 μ mol H2PtCl6, 5 μ mol IrCl3, 8 μ mol CuCl2, 7 μ mol InCl3, 20 μ mol Ga(NO3)3, 7 μ mol Pb(NO3)2] and additives (4 μ mol KSCN, 210 μ mol benzoic acid, and 300 μ L of DMF) were dissolved in 10 mL of H2O. Then, 10 mg of OH-XC-72 was dispersed in this solution. The mixture was transferred to a 25 mL Teflon-lined stainless autoclave and heated at 170 ° C for 4 h under stirring. The product was collected by centrifugation and washed with H2O. It is worth noting that while this synthesis can overcome the huge reduction potential gaps between different metals and promote their effective coreduction, the reduction rates of the metals still vary to some extent, depending on their individual reduction potentials. Since Ga has the lowest standard reduction potential among the six metals, a higher concentration of Ga precursor is introduced to achieve reduction rates comparable to those of the other metal precursors. The conditions for synthesizing different types of carbon-supported HEA and few-element alloy nanoparticles are presented in Table S2.

Electrochemical Measurements. Electrochemical measurements were conducted on a CHI 760E electrochemical workstation (Shanghai Chenhua) with a three-electrode system. A glass carbon electrode (0.196 cm 2 ), a Pt foil (1 × 1 cm 2 ), and an Ag/AgCl (3 M) electrode were used as the working, counter, and reference electrodes, respectively. All potentials were converted into values with respect to a RHE. Commercial Pt/C (JM, 20 wt % Pt/XC72R) was used as a reference catalyst. An ink of the catalyst was prepared by ultrasonically mixing the catalyst with H2O, isopropanol, and 5 wt % Nafion (volume ratio, 1:1:0.004) for 1 h. Then, a specific volume of the ink containing 1.6 μ g of Pt was dropped onto the working electrode and dried in air. CV curves were recorded in N 2-saturated 0.5 M H2SO4 in the potential range of 0.05 -1.1 V at a sweep rate of 50 mV s -1 . The electrochemically active surface area (ECSA) was calculated according to the charges associated with the hydrogen underpotential deposition in the region of 0.05 -0.4 V after double-layer correction (reference value: 210 C cm -2 ). The MOR was performed in N2-saturated 1 M CH3OH + 0.5 M H2SO4 at a scan rate of 50 mV s -1 . The chronoamperometric analysis was conducted at 0.8 V vs RHE for 5000 s. Accelerated durability tests were carried out in 1 M CH3OH+ 0.5 M H2SO4 between 0.05 and 1.2 V vs RHE at a scan rate of 100 mV s -1 for 1000 cycles. To obtain CO stripping voltammograms, the working electrode was held at 0.1 V for 30 min in CO-saturated 0.5 M H2SO4, followed by N2 purging for 30 min and a potential scan from 0.05 to 1.1 V at 20 mV s -1 .

Analyses of Intermediates and Products Involved in HEA Nanoparticle Synthesis. After the synthesis of HEA nanoparticles, the nanoparticles were collected by centrifugation, and the supernatant was extracted with chloroform and analyzed with GC-MS (Agilent 8890-7000D) to examine the liquid products involved in the HEA nanoparticle synthesis. The gaseous products were extracted from the upper chamber of the reaction container and analyzed using GC (SHIMADZU HS-10/GC-2010pro) and MS (Pfeiffer Vacuum OmniStar GSD 320). All gas samples were introduced into the mass spectrometer in equal volumes. To capture H · , DMPO was introduced into the typical reaction system. After completion of the reaction, the autoclave was quenched to room temperature, and the solution was quickly withdrawn and analyzed by EPR.

Characterizations. Low-magnification TEM was performed on a Hitachi HT-7700 microscope, operated at 90 kV. High-resolution TEM images and EDS mappings were acquired on a Talos F200XTEM microscope operated at 200 kV. C s-corrected HAADF-STEM images and EELS mappings were obtained on a JEM-ARM300F microscope (JEOL, 300 kV) equipped with cold FEG and double spherical aberration correctors, ADF/BF/SAAF detectors, and a 965 GIF Quantum ER spectrometer with a direct detection camera (K2). EDS elemental analysis was measured on a ZEISS EVO10 SEM, operated at 20 kV. XRD patterns were acquired on a Rigaku SmartLab Powder X-ray diffractometer equipped with Cu K α radiation and a D/ TEX Ultra detector. ICP-MS was performed on a NexION 350D. XPS data were collected on a Thermo Fisher ESCALAB Xi+ spectrometer equipped with monochromatic Al K α radiation.

DFT Calculations. DFT calculations were performed using the VASP package with the projector augmented wave (PAW) method, 73 employing an energy cutoff of 400 eV to account for core -valence interactions. All calculations utilized the Perdew -Burke -Ernzerhof (PBE) 74 exchange -correlation functional, including D3 van der Waals correction. 75 The structures of all MOR intermediates were fully relaxed until the final force on each atom was less than 0.01 eV Å -1 . The self-consistent electronic step was considered converged when the change of total energy and change of eigenvalues between two steps were both smaller than 10 -6 eV. For the optimization of the crystals, reciprocal space was sampled using the 3 × 3 × 1 Γ -centered Monkhorst -Pack sampled k-points. 76 Bottom 2 layers of structures were fixed. In a pure Pt metallic periodic structure, the primary adsorption sites can be categorized into four main types: face-centered cubic (fcc), hexagonal close-packed (hcp), top, and bridge sites. 77,78 Consequently, the adsorption energies have been calculated exclusively for these four distinct sites. For the search of adsorption sites of HEA, we consider four sites near each surface atom, include top site, hcp site, fcc site and bridge site. In order to obtain the surface * OH and * CO adsorption energy efficiently on HEA systems, 1 × 1 × 1 K -points were used and all atoms in the substrate were fixed. Subsequently, high-precision optimizations were conducted using a 3 × 3 × 1 Γ -centered Monkhorst -Pack grid, relaxing the top two substrate layers while maintaining fixed positions for the remaining atoms to ensure accurate surface adsorption energy determination. The free energy of structure ( G ) was given by G = E 0 + ZPE -TS , where the potential energy ( E 0 ) of each adsorbed state and gas molecule were yielded from DFT calculation, zero-point energy (ZPE) and the entropy ( S ) corrections were also obtained from VASP kit. 79
