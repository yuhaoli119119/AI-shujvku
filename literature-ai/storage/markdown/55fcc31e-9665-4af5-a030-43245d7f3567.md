Fast and ef /uniFB01 cient lithium-ion transport is crucial for high-performance rechargeable batteries but remains poorly understood in complex alloy anodes due to the coexistence of multiple competing diffusion mechanisms. A key challenge is disentangling how defect type, local coordination, and structural connectivity collectively govern migration barriers, which has so far limited the development of generalizable design rules. Here, we develop neuroevolution potentials (NEPs), a machine-learning framework trained to near-density functional theory (DFT) accuracy, providing both reliability and transferability for large-scale simulations of Li diffusion in Li-In and Li-Sn alloys. Using NEPs, we identify three primary factors controlling Li mobility: the dominant defect carrier (Li vacancy, Li interstitial, or metal vacancy), the connectivity of low-barrier diffusion pathways, and the local atomic environment, with migration distance and charge redistribution as the most critical determinants of diffusion barriers. Li diffusion shows strong structural inheritance across intermetallics, where the crystal backbone dictates long-range connectivity and chemical composition /uniFB01 ne-tunes local behavior. The favorable diffusion structural frameworks revealed by NEPs can be extended to other Lirich intermetallics, enabling predictive screening of fast-diffusing phases. This work establishes a predictive framework for designing next-generation alloy anodes with high ionic conductivity.

Rechargeable lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) are indispensable to portable electronics, electric vehicles, and grid-scale energy storage, forming a cornerstone of the global transition toward sustainable energy 1,2 . Achieving higherenergy density, faster charging, and longer cycle life critically depends on the mobility of lithium ions within electrode materials. Beyond thermodynamics and interfacial chemistry, lithium diffusion represents a fundamental rate-limiting factor governing power performance and overall device ef /uniFB01 ciency 3,4 . Elucidating the atomistic mechanisms of Li migration is therefore essential for optimizing electrode design and extending the frontiers of energy storage technologies 5,6 .

Extensive studies have characterized lithium diffusion across various electrode classes, including from intercalation-type oxides, alloying-based materials, and conversion-based systems 7 -10 . These investigations collectively reveal that Li kinetics are highly sensitive to the local chemical and structural environment, which depends on factors, such as composition 11 , phase 12 , and lithiation state 13 . Consequently, developing a systematic methodology that links local chemical and structural environments to diffusion pathways and migration barriers across different material classes is valuable for identifying the key factors governing Li transport and guiding electrode design. For intercalation-type oxide electrodes and oxide-type solid-state electrolytes, previous studies have established that parameters, such as the valence of the transition-metal cation, the inter-cation distances, local coordination geometry, and polyhedral connectivity can affect lithium-ion mobility 14 -16 . In contrast, analogous studies in alloy-based systems remain relatively limited, leaving a gap in transferable insights across material classes. Advancing understanding in this area would enable more predictive tuning of ion transport behavior and support the rational design of high-performance alloy electrodes.

Atomistic simulations provide indispensable insight into lithium transport at the microscopic level 17,18 . The nudged elastic band (NEB) method can determine migration barriers along prede /uniFB01 ned paths, while heuristic approaches, such as bond-valence and Ewald-energy analyses can map plausible routes but cannot quantify conductivity 19,20 . ## Results

## Machine-Learning Potential Development

MLPs for Li-In and Li-Sn alloys are developed using the NEP framework (parameters in Table S1) implemented in GPUMD 41,42 . In this framework, the total energy of the system, U , is expressed as a sum of atomic site energies U i, which depend on the local environment of each atom within a /uniFB01 nite cutoff radius. By construction, this approach allows ef /uniFB01 cient scaling to larger systems while preserving DFT-level accuracy, where the reliability of predictions depends critically on the comprehensiveness and representativeness of the training dataset. The initial training datasets are generated by combiningtwocomplementarystrategies(Fig.1a).First,equilibriumAIMD simulations are performed at temperatures from 300 to 1800 K on known structures obtained from the Materials Project and OQMD databases 43,44 , with snapshots sampled at regular time intervals to capture thermally accessible atomic con /uniFB01 gurations. Second, isotropic structural perturbations are introduced by scaling lattice parameters by factors of 0.8 -1.2, thereby sampling non-equilibrium con /uniFB01 gurations to enhance coverage of the potential energy landscape. Principal component analysis (PCA) 45 is used to evaluate the diversity and structural distribution of the constructed dataset. As shown in Fig. 1a, projection onto a two-dimensional PCA space reveals that local atomic environments for each composition are well represented; however, the sampled con /uniFB01 gurations occupy a relatively narrow region of the potential energy landscape. This indicates that, although the initial dataset captures key structural variations, additional sampling is required to achieve broader and more robust coverage of the con /uniFB01 gurational space.

To further enhance structural diversity, especially for con /uniFB01 gurations relevant to Li diffusion and phase transformations, the NEP model is integrated with CALYPSO for random structure prediction in an iterative work /uniFB02 ow 46 . This integration leverages the excellent balance of NEP between DFT-level accuracy and computational ef /uniFB01 ciency 23,47,48 . A diverse structural dataset is generated using CALYPSO, whose ef /uniFB01 cient global search capabilities enhance the coverage and robustness of the NEP training process 28,49 . The initial dataset shown in the left panel of Fig. 1a is used as the training set to constructthe NEP model, which is subsequently employed for structural relaxations in the /uniFB01 rst generation of CALYPSO exploration. In each cycle, 2500 candidate structures are generated, and the most distinct ones are selected using farthest-point sampling, a strategy that maximizes geometric diversity while minimizing redundancy 42 . These selected structures are subsequently evaluated by DFT calculation to obtain energies, forces, and virials, which are added to the training set before retraining the NEP model (the right panel of Fig. 1a). This process is repeated until the model accurately predicted all candidate structures, resulting in /uniFB01 nal datasets of 2612 Li-In and 3106 Li-Sn structures. PCA analysis con /uniFB01 rms a signi /uniFB01 cant increase in con /uniFB01 gurational coverage compared with the initial datasets.

Validation of the NEP models is performed against DFT calculations. Toevaluate the performance of the /uniFB01 nal NEPmodel,we /uniFB01 rst check its ability to reproduce DFT results. Figure 1b and S1 shows good agreement between NEP and DFT results for energies, forces, and virials across a wide range of compositions and structures. For Li-In (Li-Sn) alloys, the root-mean-square errors (RMSEs) for energies, forces, and virial tensors in the training set are 11(16)meV/atom,64(67)meV/Å,and45(71)meV/atom,respectively.For anindependenttest set of 2000 randomly generated Li-In (Li-Sn) structures, the RMSE are 7 (8) meV/atom, 46 (64) meV/Å, and 42 (46) meV/atom. Furthermore, NEP-MD simulations reproduce AIMD atomic trajectories, radial distribution functions (RDF), phonon spectra, energy-volume relations, and lattice parameters (deviations ≤ 0.9%), con /uniFB01 rming that NEP reliably captures both local bonding and long-range structural correlations (Fig. S2 and Table S2). Collectively, these results demonstrate that the developed NEP models are accurate, transferable, and suitable for largescale MD simulations aimed at systematically exploring Li diffusion pathways, migration mechanisms, and kinetics in complex Li-In and Li-Sn alloys.

All NEP-predicted structures within this range are re-optimized using DFT, following established practices in MLP-based structure prediction 28,51 . ## Atomic-Scale Mechanisms and Diffusion Pathways of Li in Li-In and Li-Sn Alloys

Understanding the atomic-scale mechanisms governing Li-ion transport in Li-In and Li-Sn alloys is crucial for elucidating their electrochemical behavior and optimizing battery performance. Li diffusion in these systems primarily occurs via interstitial and vacancy-mediated migration, each associated with distinct dominant carriers. To clarify their contributions, we performed NEP-MD simulations under three representative conditions-M (In/Sn) vacancies, Li vacancies, and Li interstitials-using a uniform 1% defect concentration. To validate the reliability of the NEP, we compared the Li self-diffusion coef /uniFB01 cients and activation energies obtained from pulsed /uniFB01 eld gradient nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy (PFG-NMR) measurements 57 of LiIn with those predicted by the NEP (Fig. 3a and S11). The calculated Li-vacancy diffusion coef /uniFB01 cient (6.0 ± 0.2 × 10 -7 cm 2 /s) and activation energy (0.05 ± 0.002 eV) at 300 K are in good agreement with the experimental values (5.5 ± 1.0 × 10 -7 cm 2 /s and 0.068 ± 0.037 eV). Besides, incorporating the uncertainty does not alter the mechanistic interpretation: Li vacancies consistently remain the dominant carriers of LiIn, and the relative ranking of diffusion performance among the materials is preserved.

## Discussion

Our analysis demonstrates that Li-ion transport in Li-In and Li-Sn alloys is governed by three interrelated factors: the Li content, the local atomic environment, and the connectivity of low-energy migration pathways. The connectivity of the Li sublattice network determines the dominant diffusion mechanism: isolated Li sites promote interstitial-mediated diffusion, whereas continuous Li networks favor vacancy-mediated diffusion. Local atomic environments, including Li-Li distances and charge redistribution along hopping paths, modulate migration barriers for individual steps. For instance, in Li 5 In4, diffusion along well-connected 2-D pathways results in highLimobility(D ≈ 6.3 × 10 -7 cm 2 /s), whereas in Li 3 In2, reliance on higherbarrier, less-connected paths reduces the diffusion coef /uniFB01 cient by more than an order of magnitude. These results clarify that effective long-range Li mobility arises from the interplay between local barriers and the connectivity of accessible low-energy pathways.

Chemical composition (Li-In vs. Li-Sn) /uniFB01 ne-tunes local migration barriers without fundamentally altering the dominant diffusion mechanism, which is determined by Li network connectivity. COHP analyses indicate that Li-Sn interactions are slightly stronger than Li-In, leading to marginally higher local barriers, consistent with the modest differences in diffusion coef /uniFB01 cients between analogous phases. In high-Li phases of Li-Sn (e.g., Li7Sn2), dense Li chains induce cooperative multi-ion hopping, enabling interstitial-dominated diffusion even at high Li content and maximizing ionic mobility.

Totest the generality of these insights, we examined chemically related Li-rich intermetallics with comparable Li content, including LiIn-type (LiGa, Li2SbAu, Li2AgBi, Li2AuBi), Li7Sn2-type (Li7Ge2, Li7Si2), Li15Sn4type (Li 15Ge4, Li 15 Si 4 ), and Li22Sn5-type (Li 22Ge5, Li 22 Si 5 ) frameworks.

Overall, our study establishes three central principles governing Li transport in complex alloy anodes: (i) the connectivity of the Li sublattice network determines the dominant carrier type, (ii) the local atomic environment sets migration barriers for individual hopping steps, and (iii) continuous networks of favorable migration paths enable high long-range mobility. By identifying structural con /uniFB01 gurations that consistently support fast Li diffusion, this framework provides a mechanistic basis for screening Li-rich intermetallics, facilitating the rational design of next-generation alloy electrodes with enhanced ionic conductivity.

## DFT Calculation

In this study, DFT calculations are performed using the Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package (VASP) to obtain energies, atomic forces, and virial tensors required for training NEPs 63 . The projector augmented-wave (PAW) method is employed in conjunction with the Perdew-BurkeErnzerhof (PBE) exchange-correlation functional within the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) 64 . A plane-wave energy cutoff of 500 eV is used. The Brillouin zone is sampled using a Monkhorst-Pack k-point grid with a spacing of 0.168 Å -1 . The energy convergence criterion for static calculations is set to 10 -5 eV, and the force convergence threshold is 0.01 eV/Å to ensure high accuracy in energy, forces, and structural details. Phonon calculations are carried out using the /uniFB01 nite displacement method 65 , implemented in the PHONOPY code 66 . COHP 61 calculations are performed with the LOBSTER software package. ## AIMD Calculations

AIMDsimulations in the canonical ensemble (NVT) are performed using a Nosé-Hoover thermostat to control temperature, ranging from 300 to 1800 K and are used to generate the initial training dataset. The minimal supercell approach is adopted to ensure that the lattice vectors exceeded 8 Å in all directions. A time step of 1 fs is used, with simulation durations of at least 5 ps. For AIMD simulations, only the Γ -point is used for Brillouin zone sampling.

## NEP-Based Structure Search

During the CALYPSO-NEP structural exploration to generate dataset, we performed variable- stoichiometry searches within LixMy (M=In or Sn; x = 1 -20; y = 1 -20) and the total number of atoms capped at 40 per unit cell. In each iteration, 2500 low-energy structures were generated and then reduced by farthest-point sampling to retain only representative ones. This iterative process was repeated until the NEP model achieved full consistency with DFT energies and forces.

After obtaining a high-accuracy NEP, both CALYPSO 46 andUSPEX 67 were employed to explore stable and metastable phases in the Li-In and LiSnsystems. For /uniFB01 xed-stoichiometry searches in CALYPSO, the simulation cell size was set to 1-4 formula units. After the /uniFB01 rst generation, 50% of the low-energy structures were selected to construct the next generation via the particle-swarm optimization (PSO) algorithm. In most cases, the structural search was terminated after approximately 4000 -5000 structures had been generated.

The USPEX code combined with the NEP is used to predict stable phasesintheLi-InandLi-Snalloysystems.Avariable-compositionsearchis /uniFB01 rst performed, where the /uniFB01 rst generation consisted of 60 structures, and the number of atoms per primitive cell is limited to between 2 and 36. For the generation of subsequent structures (each generation containing 200 structures), 40% of the lowest-energy structures from the previous generation are optimized using variation operators-including heredity, softmutation, transmutation, and lattice mutation-while the remaining 60% are randomly generated. This process is repeated for 101 generations.

Based on the convex hull results from the variable-composition search, a /uniFB01 xed-composition search is then conducted on more than 60 low-energy Li-In/Sn stoichiometries. Each generation consisted of 60 structures, and the search is repeated for 50 generations. In each generation, 50% of the structures are randomly generated and the remaining 50% are produced through variation operators applied to existing low-energy con /uniFB01 gurations.

## Large-Scale Molecular Dynamic (MD) Simulations

To ensure that the NEP potential accurately describes Li diffusion in the predicted phases, MD simulations are performed, with a heating phase (from 0 to 650 K, lasting 1 ns) followed by equilibration phase (lasting 1 ns). During the simulation, structures are continuously collected, and con /uniFB01 gurations with the largest energy and force deviations are added to the training dataset.

The diffusion coef /uniFB01 cient calculation are carried out using the NEPbased Large-scale Atomic/Molecular Massively Parallel Simulator (LAMMPS) software package 68 . The lattice constants of the structures used for ion diffusion coef /uniFB01 cient calculations is exceeding 30 Å. The defective structures are generated using the Atomsk tool by randomly adding or removing approximately 1% of the atoms in the atomic structure 69 . The simulations are conducted in the temperature range from 300 to 500 K, with a 50 K interval. For each temperature, the system is heated for 1 ns and equilibrated for 5 ns under the NPT ensemble with a time step of 1 fs. The simulation results are visualized and analyzed using the Open Visualization Tool (OVITO) and Visual Molecular Dynamics (VMD) software 70,71 . ## Gradient Boosting Regression (GBR)

In this study, GBR model is employed to analyze the key factors in /uniFB02 uencing local Li diffusion.
