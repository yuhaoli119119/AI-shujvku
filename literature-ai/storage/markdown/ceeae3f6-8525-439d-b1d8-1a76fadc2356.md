Abstract 
Rational design of lithium (Li) metal  anodes is critical for achieving uniform Li 
deposition and long -term cycling stability. Here, we report a sequential atomic layer 
deposition of lithiophilic ZnO and Li 2O coatings on 3D carbon paper to construct a 
vertically graded bilayer artificial inter face. The underlying ZnO -derived LiZn/Li 2O 
domains act as mixed ionic /electronic conductors and provide stable nucleation sites 
for Li deposition. The high-resistance Li2O top layer suppresses electron tunneling and 
increases the nucleation size. This synergistic design directs bottom-up Li growth, fully 
exploiting the internal voids of the 3D sc affold, suppressing dendrite formation . As a 
result, the Li@CP/Z6L1||LCO cell achieves 83.1% capacity retention after 200 cycles 
at −20 °C (~4000 h), while the Li@CP/Z6L1||LFP cell maintains 96.4% capacity after 
500 cycles, with only 0.0072% capacity loss per cycle.  This work demonstrates a 
rational bilayer interface design that leverages vertical conductivity gradients and 
interfacial chemistry to achieve high -performance, durable, and low -temperature-
tolerant lithium metal anodes.

1. Introduction 
Lithium metal batteries (LMBs) have emerged as a transformative energy storage 
technology, leveraging the unparalleled theoretical capacity (3860 mAh g−1) and ultra-
low electrochemical potential (−3.04 V vs. SHE) of lithium metal anodes (LMA)[1-3]. 
However, the uncontrollable growth of Li dendrites and drastic volume fluctuations 
during cycling remain formidable barriers to practical applications. These issues lead 
to rapid capacity decay, electrolyte depletion, and catastrophic safety hazards.  
Achieving dendrite-free Li deposition is esse ntial for ensuring the safe operation and 
long-term cycling stability of LMBs. The root cause of the unlimited volume expansion 
lies in the host -free planar deposition of Li metal[4, 5] . In LMB systems, planar 
electrodes tend to generate sharp protrusions during the initial stages of deposition, 
which locally intensify Li+ flux. The uneven distribution of Li+ flux directly triggers the 
formation of Li dendrites. To address this problem, the rational design of Li metal hosts 
that provide ample accommodation space and promote uniform Li+ flux distribution 
has emerged as a promising strategy [6, 7]. Three-dimensional (3D) current collectors 
with high surface area not only reduce local current density to homogenize Li 
deposition but also mitigate volume expansion induced by Li metal. To date, commonly 
reported 3D current collectors, including metallic foams [8-10], carbon nanotubes[11-
13], carbon fibers [14-16], and 3D MXene -based architectures [17-19], have been 
extensively explored to promote uniform Li deposition. 
Carbon-based hosts are regarded as highly promising candidates for dendrite -free 
LMA due to their chemical stability, excellent electronic conductivity, and lightweight

3 
 
properties[20-22]. For example, Cui et al. reported a layered Li -rGO electrode 
fabricated by infusing molten Li into the nanoscale gaps of lithiophilic reduced 
graphene oxide (rGO) [23]. The periodic stacking of nanosized Li and rGO layers 
provides a robust scaffold for Li plating and stripping, effectively mitigating volume 
fluctuations dur ing cycling. Similar to this strategy, previous studies have typically 
employed molten Li infusion to prepare composite Li anodes, which inevitably leads to 
safety concerns, interfacial inhomogeneity, and imprecise control of the Li content [24-
26]. In addition, most carbon materials exhibit relatively weak interactions with Li, 
resulting in high nucleation overpotentials [27, 28]. As a result, Li tends to deposit 
laterally along the top surface rather than penetrating vertically into the porous structure. 
This inefficient space utilization exacerbates dendrite formation and significantly limits 
the advantages of 3D frameworks. 
Recent studies have demonstrated that rationally designing the conductivity and 
lithiophilicity gradients of 3D current collectors  can effectively address the challenge 
of Li growth concentrated at the top surface [29, 30]. For instance, Wu et al. applied 
ZnO and Au coatings on the top and bottom surfaces of copper foam, respectively [8]. 
This hierarchical structure enabled a gradual e nhancement of lithiophilicity and 
conductivity from top to bottom, providing sufficient driving force for the downward 
migration of Li⁺. Consequently, Li was synchronously deposited throughout the entire 
3D space. In addition, Oyakhire and co-workers systematically investigated Cu current 
collectors modified with nanofilms of different resistivities to explore the correlation 
between substrate electrical properties and Li deposition behavior[31]. They found that

high-resistivity surfaces favored the formati on of larger Li nuclei, which effectively 
reduced the specific surface area of the deposits, thereby mitigating electrolyte 
consumption and suppressing subsequent dead Li accumulation. However, rationally 
designing artificial coatings to induce bottom-up Li deposition within 3D hosts remains 
a significant challenge. 
Herein, we propose a bilayer artificial interface engineering strategy by sequentially 
depositing ZnO and Li 2O layers via ALD on carbon paper (CP) , denoted as 
CP/ZnO/Li2O (The optimized sample is referred to as CP/Z6L1), and further fabricating 
composite Li anodes by cold-pressing with 50 µm Li foil. The underlying ZnO-derived 
LiZn/Li2O domains act as mixed ionic /electronic conductors , which  provide low -
energy nucleation sites and facilitate the rapid lateral electron transport, guiding the 
growth of Li metal along the Li (110) crystal plane parallel to the substrate [32]. 
Moreover, Li2O functions as an electron -insulating yet ion -conductive m ediator, 
effectively suppressing electron tunneling at the LiZn/Li 2O and Li 2O interface while 
enhancing Li+ transport[33, 34]. Density functional theory (DFT) calculations confirm 
that the terminal oxygen groups of Li2O act as electron donors, exhibiting nucleophilic 
characteristics that promote the preferential reduction of TFSI − anions to form a LiF -
rich SEI layer[3]. The constructed vertical -gradient bilayer artificial interface guides 
bottom-up Li growth, fully exploiting the internal voids of the 3D s caffold and 
suppressing dendrite formation. This synergistic design enhances the utilization 
efficiency of the entire 3D framework for Li deposition, thereby alleviating volume 
fluctuations of the anode. As a result, the optimized CP/Z6L1@Li symmetric cell

demonstrates an ultralong cycling life of 2 900 hours with an overpotential below 10

the Li@CP/Z6L1||LFP full cell exhibited excellent cycling stability, retaining 96.4% of 
its capacity after 500 cycles at 1 C, corresponding to an average capacity loss of only 
0.0072% per cycle. 
2. Results and discussion 
During the ALD process, diethylzinc and H 2O precursors were sequentially 
introduced onto the carbon paper (CP) surface to deposit a ZnO layer, followed by Li 
tert-butoxide treatment to form an overlying Li 2O coating (Fig. 1a). The synergistic 
lithiophilic nature of ZnO and Li 2O collectively facilitates uniform Li nucleation and 
deposition. To quantitatively assess lithiophilicity, density functional theory (DFT) 
calculations were performed to determine the adsorption energies of Li atoms on 
various surfaces. The calculated adsorption energies for Li (110), ZnO (101), and Li2O 
(111) surfaces were −3.731, −5.364, and −4.023 eV , respectively, in comparison to 
−1.400 eV on pristine carbon paper (Fig. 1b). The more negative adsorption energies 
on ZnO and Li2O indicate significantly enhanced Li affinity, suggesting preferential Li+ 
nucleation on these modified surfaces. Furthermore, differential charge density analysis 
revealed substantial electron accumulation at the Li/ZnO and Li/Li 2O interfaces (Fig. 
S1), indicating strong interfacial electronic interac tions that contribute to the

stabilization of Li deposition.

To systematically tailor the coating composition and structure, the thickness of the 
Li2O layer was precisely adjusted by controlling the number of atomic layer deposition 
(ALD) cycles, yielding a series of 3D current collectors designated as CP/Z6, 
CP/Z6L0.5, CP/Z6L1, and CP/Z6L2. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) revealed 
conformal coating coverage on carbon fibers, with surface morphology evolving from 
nanoparticles to nanosheets as the Li2O thickness increased (Fig. 2a,  d and Fig. S 2). 
Digital images revealed a consistent color transition from gray -black to blue, 
confirming homogeneous surface modification. Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy

cross-sectional regions of the carbon paper (Fig. 2b, c and Fig. S3). X-ray diffraction 
(XRD) patterns exhibited three distinct peaks in the 30–40° range (Fig. 2e), indexed to 
the hexagonal ZnO phase (JCPDS No. 36-1451), while the absence of Li2O diffraction 
peaks suggests its amorphous or ultrathin character. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy 
(XPS) analysis further confirmed the coating composition (Fig. 2g, h and Fig. S4). The 
Zn 2p spectrum showed peaks at 1044.08 eV and 1021.03 eV , corresponding to Zn 2p1/2 
and Zn 2p3/2, respectively, with a 23.1 eV energy separation characteristic of Zn2+. The 
O 1s spectrum was deconvoluted into two components at 530.0 eV and 531.1 eV , 
attributed to lattice oxygen (Zn –O/Li–O) and surface -adsorbed oxygen species, 
respectively. These results confirm the successful deposition of ZnO and Li 2O 
nanoparticles on carbon fibers via ALD.  Four-point probe measurements showed a 
slight reduction in electronic conductivity from 1.3  104 S m−1 for pristine CP to 1.23 
 104 S m−1 after 200 ALD cycles, highlighting the electron -insulating nature of Li2O 
(Fig. 2f) . Ionic co nductivity measurements revealed that the ZnO coating slightly 
decreased the ionic conductivity of CP, whereas the Li2O coating significantly enhanced 
it (Fig. 2i and Fig. S5). These results demonstrate that Li2O serves as an effective ionic 
conductor while maintaining electronic insulation, thereby contributing to the vertical 
conductivity gradient across the bilayer interface.

continued, the Zn (101) peak gradually diminished, while diffraction signals at 40.8 °,

Although Li2O forms during ZnO reduction, no Li2O diffraction peaks were observed, 
indicating that the formed phase is amorphous or low cr ystallinity [24]. 
To predict the synergistic effect of the ZnO/Li2O bilayer, COMSOL simulations were 
performed to compare Li deposition behavior on CP/Z6 and CP/Z6L1 electrodes  (Fig. 
3b, c). In the CP/Z6 system, Li nucleation and growth were strongly localized, with 
lithiophilic sites driving protrusion formation and producing pronounced heterogeneity 
in the Li⁺ concentration field, including depletion at protrusion tips and accumulation 
in recessed regions. Such gradients are expected to aggravate morphological instability 
at the electrode/electrolyte interface. In contrast, the ZnO/Li 2O bilayer markedly 
homogenized the Li⁺ flux: the Li2O top layer facilitated uniform redistribution of Li ⁺, 
while the ZnO-derived LiZn/Li2O domains at the bottom provided stable sites for ion–
electron recombination. This vertically graded architecture transformed Li ⁺ transport

dominated three-dimensional process, thereby promoting spatially uniform Li 
nucleation and the subsequent growth of a dense and smooth Li layer. 
Li deposition morphology and thickness evolution under ZnO/Li 2O coatings were 
systematically studied by plating Li onto 3D frameworks at different areal capacities  
(Fig. 3d-g and Fig. S6). At 3 mAh cm−2, bare Li formed coral-like dendrites, while Li 
on CP accumulated unevenly on the surface. In contrast, CP/Z6L1 enabled ultrathin, 
uniform Li layers with visible carbon fiber s, indicating favorable nucleation. Even at 
10 mAh cm−2, CP/Z6L1 maintained dendrite-free, compact Li, minimizing electrolyte 
contact and parasitic reactions, whereas Li on CP formed loose dendrites and isolated 
islands, causing inactive Li accumulation. Cross-sectional SEM showed that depositing 
10 mAh cm−2 of Li on a planar Li substrate increased the thickness by ~140 µm, which 
is already comparable to the compressed CP thickness.  Moreover, Li preferentially 
deposited on the top surface of lithiophobic CP, increasing the thickness from ~170 µm 
to ~230 µm.  For lithiophilic CP/Z6L1, thickness remained almost unchanged, 
demonstrating that the ZnO/Li 2O coating guided bottom -up deposition and efficient 
utilization of the 3D scaffold, reducing volume fluctuations during cycling. Nucleation-
size distributions were analyzed at a fixed current density of 0.5 mA cm −2 with a 
deposition capacity of 5 mAh cm−2 for Li, bare CP, and CP/Z6L1 electrodes (Fig. S8). 
The SEM images and statistical histograms show that Li nuclei formed on CP/Z6L1 are 
significantly larger than those on Li and bare CP. This is attributed to the higher 
interfacial resistance of the Li 2O layer, which reduces the density of nucleation sites

To investigate the evolution of SEI composition, XPS depth profiling was performed 
on cycled electrodes (Fig. S14-S17). As shown in Fig. 5a, d, the C 1s spectra consisted 
of C–C (284.8 eV), C–O (286.4 eV), C=O (288.5 eV), Li2CO3 (290.2 eV), C–F2 (290.9 
eV), and C–F (292.9 eV), originating from electrolyte decomposition. After 60 s of Ar+ 
sputtering, carbonaceous species were significantly reduced on the CP/Z6L1 surface, 
with r esidual CO32– peaks remaining, whereas stronger organic signals persisted on 
CP/Z6 and bare Li. High -resolution F 1s spectra (Fig. 5 b, e) revealed the coexistence 
of organic C–F (688.8 eV) and inorganic LiF (685.2 eV). A pronounced LiF signal was 
observed on CP/Z6L1, which further increased after sputtering, while C–F components 
disappeared. In contrast, CP/Z6 contained less LiF and retained noticeable C–F signals. 
Quantitative analysis confirmed that the CP/Z6L1 electrode contained the highest 
fraction of inorganic species, dominated by LiF and Li 2CO3, whereas CP/Z6 and bare 
Li exhibited higher proportions of organic species  (Fig. 5c, f). These results indicate 
that the bilayer ZnO/Li2O interface promotes more complete decomposition of TFSI ⁻ 
and facilitates the formation of a LiF -rich SEI, consistent with improved interfacial 
stability and Li ⁺ transport. In addition, SEM observations after 30 plating/stripping 
cycles at 5 mAh cm⁻² revealed that bare CP was fully covered by a dense Li layer with 
filament-like deposits and thick residual Li after stripping. In contrast, CP/Z6L1 
exhibited uniform, dendrite -free deposition, with Li preferentially filling the internal 
scaffold and leaving minimal residual Li after stripping, thus exposing the 3D

In ether-based electrolytes, the low LUMO energy of LiTFSI (−1.61 eV) favors its 
preferential reduction and contributes to the formation of LiF -rich SEI. To further 
elucidate the interfacial mechanism, DFT calculations were carried out [35]. The 
adsorption energy of TFSI ⁻ on bare Li (−2.8 eV) was significantly less negative than 
those on Li 2O (111) (−3.565 eV) and LiZn  (111) (−5.185 eV), indicating stronger 
binding of TFSI⁻ at the coated interfaces (Fig. 5g). Differential charge density analyses 
revealed charge transfers of 1.04 electrons from Li2O to TFSI⁻ (Fig. 5h), consistent with 
electrostatic potential mapping that identified terminal O–Li groups in Li2O as electron-
rich sites with strong nucleophilicity  (Fig. 5i). These regions readily interact with  the 
positively polarized S=O and C –F bonds in TFSI ⁻, thereby promoting its stepwise 
decomposition into LiF [3, 36]. In this dual-layer artificial interface, the Li2O top layer 
acts as an ionic conductor and electronic insulator, blocking electron penetration while 
permitting Li ⁺ transport, whereas the underlying LiZn layer provides mixed 
ionic/electronic conductivity that facilitates Li ⁺/electron recombination (Fig. 5j). This 
vertical conductivity gradient not only guides bottom -up Li deposition and enable s 
uniform, dendrite-free growth, but also promotes selective decomposition of TFSI⁻ into 
LiF, as further supported by XPS analysis.

of relaxation times (DRT) analy sis, was utilized to track the dynamic evolution of

3. Conclusions 
In summary, ZnO/Li2O bilayer artificial interface on 3D carbon paper forms a 
vertically graded artificial interface that synergistically regulates Li nucleation and 
growth. The underlying ZnO -derived LiZn/Li 2O domains provide mixed ionic -
electronic conductivity and stable nucleation sites, promoting directional Li deposition, 
while the high -resistance Li 2O top layer reduces the nucleation site density and 
facilitates radial Li diffusion. The formation of larger Li nuclei decreases the effective 
surface area, mitigating electr olyte consumption and dead -Li accumulation. The 
vertical conductivity gradient of the bilayer interface acts synergistically to guide 
uniform bottom-up Li deposition, resulting in a dense, planar Li layer. Additionally, the 
Li2O layer induces complete deco mposition of TFSI ⁻, forming a LiF -rich SEI that 
stabilizes the interface and enhances Li ⁺ transport. As a result, the Li@CP/Z6L1 
symmetric cell achieves ultrastable cycling over 2900 h with minimal polarization. Full 
cells exhibit excellent performance under low-temperature and long-term cycling, with 
the Li@CP/Z6L1||LCO cell retaining 83.1% capacity at −20 °C after 200 cycles, and 
the Li@CP/Z6L1||LFP cell maintaining 96.4% capacity over 500 cycles. This scalable 
bilayer design provides a practical strategy  for constructing functional artificial 
interphases and offers guidance for the development of high-performance lithium metal 
anodes.
